Dimanaid is the high tongue re-trued: the same roots and compounding, kept in meaning, run through one clarified phonology and a handful of grammar reforms. Every rule below is what the tools on the other tabs run on, and every form is derived live from the shared Audalad lexicon.

The writing system

A bespoke script, re-voiced

Dimanaid is written in the high tongue’s own alphabet, shown throughout the app in gold and set in Dimanaid’s own slanted hand. The roman spelling you type is a transcription; the native glyphs are the real thing.

  • One sound, one symbol. The court’s stress accents (á, é, ú) are dropped; the rebuilt verb carries those distinctions plainly instead.
  • The script has a glyph for each base letter a–z and does not draw the apostrophe.

The clarified sounds

One phonology laid over every root

Where the harbour’s Low-Audalad lets sounds slur and fall, Dimanaid sharpens them. Each high word is given a single Dimanaid form by these laws:

  • Hushed sibilants clear: sh→s, zh→z. Nashei → Nasei woman
  • Doubled letters settle to one. Naddar → Nadar wind
  • au rises to ou; ai is held open as the full glide ai (“ahy”). Razai → Razai ice (“rah-zahy”)
  • The deep vowel: every u deepens to o. Nadu → Nado noble
  • Hard k is cut clean as c. Sarek → Sarec to know
  • A trailing a closes to e; a final -ad lightens to -id. Dimainad → Dimanaid Dee·ma·nahy·id

These run automatically on every result. Nothing here is a separate dictionary: change a word in the court and its Dimanaid form changes with it.

Roots & clusterification

Kept whole from the high tongue

Dimanaid keeps the high tongue’s finest habit: new concepts are made by stacking roots into a single cluster word joined by the ’ mark, then re-voiced by the sound laws. Au (water) + dal (land) + ad (of) still builds island.

The Forge → Compound tool stacks roots for you and adds the ’ mark automatically.

Number

One plural, for everything

The court split its plural by whether a thing could breathe: -ae for the living, the prefix Yi- for the rest. Dimanaid uses a single plural -ae on everything, and keeps Yimmu (“many”) as a free quantifier.

Yiutal → Otalae houses

Possession & pronouns

One ending, plain pronouns

Possession uses a single suffix -ad (“of the…”). When it falls at the very end of a word the light-ending law surfaces it as -id:

Sor gold → Sorid golden

Pronouns start from four plain bases and take the same endings. The possessive surfaces as -id when it ends the word, and stays -ad when a plural follows:

PersonBasePossessivePlural (-ae)
1st: I / meInInid myInae we
2nd: youIrIrid yourIrae you all
3rd: he / she / theyEnoEnoid theirEnoae they
3rd inanimate: itKiKiid itsKiae they

Possessive and plural stack: Inadae = our / ours, the -ad kept inside and the plural -ae closing the word.

Negation is lifted off the tail and set free in front as Vir, so it is no longer confused with Ir “you”: sarekir → vir sarec does not know

The action-class final vowels (-i abstract, -u physical, -a emotional, and so on) are kept from the high tongue, re-voiced by the sound laws. The Conjugate tab lays out the slots for any verb.

Putting it together

Most words follow one pattern: a root (optionally clustered by ’), run through the clarified phonology, with a plain tense slot on verbs and the endings -ae (plural) and -ad / -id (possessive) stacked as needed. Rank is a free choice, not a fixed mark.

“water” · the high tongue, then the low form

Audalad belongs to the court: carved into accords, spoken slow beneath the gold, guarded word by word by the Council. Low-Audalad is that same tongue let loose on the water. It is the speech of a crowded deck, a market stall, the long dust of a trade road, quicker, softer, and answerable to no one's rank.

The tongue of the trade roads

Wherever Audalad travels to do business, it loosens. A trader calling prices over the din of a market, a deckhand shouting orders through wind and spray, a carter who passes through a dozen ports in a season: none of them has time for the court's long vowels and careful endings. Low-Audalad is what remains when the tongue is worked hard and fast, day upon day. It belongs to the sailors, dockfolk, merchants and carters who move the Empire's goods, and who will likely never set foot in a council chamber.

It has no homeland but the harbor and the road. You will hear it thickest where the water meets the stone: on wharves and quaysides, aboard the trade ships, in the warehouse rows and the open markets where a hundred bargains are struck at once. Inland it thins; in the high halls it is scarcely spoken at all. But across the breadth of the Empire it is, by simple count of mouths, the most common Audalad there is.

How it wore smooth

No one decreed Low-Audalad. It is Audalad with the corners rubbed off, the way a coin wears smooth in a thousand hands. Glided vowels run together, because a mouth in a hurry will not hold them apart. Hard sounds soften, because soft ones carry further over noise. The tails of long words fall away, because the meaning was already plain before the speaker reached them. Every one of these is a change the high tongue itself would make, were it ever set down and left to ordinary use, rather than guarded.

What the court makes of it

Scribes and nobles have long called it rough speech for rough work: a bastard tongue, unfit for an accord or the Emperor's ear. And yet it is the form of Audalad most mouths actually shape, and a high-tongue speaker can follow it without much trouble, because the bones beneath are the same, only worn. The two are not separate languages. They are one tongue, held apart by who is speaking, and by how great a hurry that speaker is in. Should the Accord that polishes the high lexicon ever turn to the low one, it would find nothing to invent, only to write down.

The three sound laws

Three regular changes turn any high-tongue word into its low form. They apply in order, and a single word may meet all three.

1 · Twin vowels become one

A glided pair of vowels collapses to a single sound. ai turns to e, and au turns to o.

Audalad → Odalad  islandAulir → Olir  snowTsaidush → Tsedush  spirit

2 · Hard sounds soften between & after vowels

A k caught between vowels, or sitting at a word's end after one, eases to g; a t in the same spot eases to d.

Murak → Murag  footSarek → Sareg  to knowShaluk → Shalug  brother

3 · Worn-off tails

A final vowel on a longer word, the kind hurried speech swallows, simply drops away, leaving the word clipped.

Nishi → Nish  childKaru → Kar  coinDalka → Dalk  rock
Short, everyday words (Au, Mi, Su, Ir) keep their shape: there is nothing to spare.

The laws run in order, so one word can meet all three. Autai “water” loses its glides to Ote, then softens between vowels to the everyday Ode.

No rank on the water

Audalad bends its pronouns to mark who outranks whom: Ori’ for the divine and the dynasty, Itu’ for a greater, Ke’ for a lesser, and no marker at all between equals. Low-Audalad keeps none of it.

There is a reason, and the harbor folk will tell it to you: on a ship the sea outranks every soul aboard, and in a market a buyer's coin speaks louder than any title. So a sailor addresses a captain, a stranger and an emperor's envoy alike with the same plain In “I,” Ir “you,” Enu “they.” Standing is heard in the words a speaker chooses, never stamped onto the grammar. To the court this is the tongue's great rudeness. To everyone else, it is simply faster.

No two ports alike

Because no Council guards it, Low-Audalad drifts. A word clipped one way in a northern harbor is clipped another way three ports down the coast; sailors carry sounds from ship to ship, and foreign traders leave their own behind in the bargain. There is no single correct spelling, only the form that gets understood before the next wave hits. What this reckoning shows is one steady hand laid over that living speech: each high word given a single low form by the three laws, so a thing said a hundred ways might at least be written one.
